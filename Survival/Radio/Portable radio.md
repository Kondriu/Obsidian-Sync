#### What is LW/MW/SW/FM?

## FM (Frequency Modulation): 

  Typically 88–108 MHz. 
  64–108 MHz (can often be toggled between 64, 76, or 87 MHz start points depending on the region).
  
  It offers high-fidelity, stereo, static-free audio, but has a limited range (line-of-sight), making it ideal for local city/regional music and news.


## SW (Shortwave):

  Generally 1.6–30 MHz.  (2.30–21.95 MHz)
  
  This band travels long distances by bouncing off the ionosphere, allowing for international broadcasting, maritime communication, and long-range, cross-border, or global communication.


## MW (Medium Wave/AM):

  Generally 530–1700 kHz. 
  522–1620 kHz (9 kHz steps) or 520–1710 kHz (10 kHz steps)
  
  Used for regional AM broadcasting, it is ideal for local, rural, and talk radio, with superior range at night.


## LW (Long Wave):

  Generally 153–279 kHz. (153–513 kHz) 
 
  Used primarily in Europe, Africa, and parts of Asia for national radio broadcasting and long-range maritime/aeronautical navigation. It is mostly nonexistent in North America.



---

**Key Differences**

- **Modulation:** MW, LW, and SW use Amplitude Modulation (AM), which can be prone to interference, while FM uses Frequency Modulation for higher quality.

- **Reception:** FM is local and high-quality; AM/MW/LW/SW are better for long distances but often lower quality.

- **Nighttime Reception:** MW and SW reception is often better at night because the signals travel longer distances.

- **Aerials:** FM uses a telescopic antenna, while MW/LW usually rely on an internal antenna requiring the radio to be rotated for better signal.


---
SSB Support & DSP Features

- **SSB (Single Sideband)**: The standard Si4734 chip **does not natively support SSB**. Radios that use it and offer SSB (like the Tecsun PL-330) often use custom firmware or the related **Si4732** chip to enable Upper Sideband (USB) and Lower Sideband (LSB) for listening to amateur radio operators.

- **DSP Advantages**: The chip provides digital filtering that allows for adjustable **AM Bandwidth** settings (typically 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0, and 6.0 kHz) to reduce interference from adjacent stations.

- **Sensitivity & Selectivity**: By digitizing the analog signal, the chip achieves high selectivity (>60 dB) and sensitivity even in compact devices
